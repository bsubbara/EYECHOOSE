    Refractive Errors

    Refractive errors are eye disorders that cause light to focus outside of the retina, producing a blurry image. They occur, because the eyeball is excessively long or short, the cornea is non-spherical, or lens has developed rigidity due to aging.

    Myopia

    Myopia, better known as “nearsightedness”, causes distant objects to appear blurry. The eyeball is too long, causing light to focus in front of the retina. It progressively worsens after onset until the achievement of eyesight maturity, with earlier onset of myopia yielding a higher prescription at the achievement of eyesight maturity. High myopia not only increases the probability of significant visual impairment by itself, but it also increases the possibility of developing glaucoma and retinal detachment, which may irreversibly impair vision, as well.

    Hyperopia

    Hyperopia, better known as farsightedness, causes nearby object to appear blurry, because the eyeball is too short, causing light to focus behind the retina. Like myopia, hyperopia is progressive, progressively worsens after onset until the achievement of eyesight maturity, with earlier onset of hyperopia yielding a higher prescription at the achievement of eyesight maturity. Moderate to high hyperopia elevates the risk of developing amblyopia and strabismus, which may precipitate visual impairment and/or blindness.

    Astigmatism

    Astigmatism causes objects at all distances to appear blurry, because the lens has a non-spherical curvature. It presents in three varieties-- myopic astigmatism, hyperopic astigmatism, and mixed astigmatism. In myopic astigmatism, lens curvature causes light to hit in front of the retina. In hyperopic astigmatism, light hits behind the retina. In mixed astigmatism, light hits as a combination of both in front of and behind the retina. Astigmatism is a progressive condition, like both myopia and hyperopia, affecting visual acuity. It can lead to amblyopia, which may affect visual acuity, even causing blindness.

    Refractive Surgeries

    Refractive surgery seeks to correct these refractive errors, through corneal remodeling, lens implantation, or lens replacement. These procedures are FDA approved for patients 21 years and older, whose eye-glasses prescriptions have remained stable for at least two years.

    Laser In-Situ Keratomileusis (LASIK)

    LASIK is the most well known and most commonly performed refractive surgery for the correction of myopia, hyperopia, and astigmatism. It is a quick procedure with minimal associated discomfort. A femtolaser or a microkeratome creates a corneal flap, under which an excimer laser reshapes the corneal tissue. Side effects and complications of this procedure present the rare risks of flap damage or loss, infection, delayed healing, undercorrection or overcorrection, irregular astigmatism, blurry vision or vision loss, scarring, corneal haze or glare, photosensitivity, and inability to wear contact lenses. Average recovery time is six to twelve hours, with most seeing clearly, regaining 90% of their vision, within 24 hours. Full effects of the surgery upon visual acuity are apparent after a month. Approximately six percent of patients choose to undergo an enhancement within ten years due to myopic regression, but LASIK is non-reversible for those patients dissatisfied with results.

    Advanced Surface Ablation

    Advanced Surface Ablation, including Photorefractive Keratectomy (PRK), Laser Epithelial Keratomileusis (LASEK), and Epithelial Laser In Situ Keratomileusis (Epi-LASIK), are the second most commonly performed refractive surgery for the correction of myopia, hyperopia, and astigmatism. In PRK, the corneal epithelium is removed, after which the Bowman membrane and corneal stroma are reshaped over the pupil using an excimer laser. In LASEK and Epi-LASIK are modifications of PRK, where an epithelial flap is created, instead of removed. A bandage contact lens is applied until the epithelium heals after each of these proceddures. The side effects and complications of thes procedures includes undercorrection or overcorrection, irregular astigmatism, blurry vision or vision loss, corneal haze or glare and halos, photosensitivity, and inability to wear contact lenses. LASEK and epi-LASIK have the additional side effects and complications of flap damage or loss. The removal of the corneal epithelium also increases pain and recovery time: Pain can last up to four days, with 90% recovery over that period. 100% recovery can be made between one to three months. Advanced Surface Ablation can be repeated to correct regression, but it cannot be reversed.

    Intraocular Lenses (IOL)

    IOL Implantation is an alternative for the correction of high myopia, high hyperopia, and astigmatism. A surgical incision is made on the cornea through which a soft lens is inserted and is placed on top of the natural lens within the eye. Side effects and complications of this procedure include infection, undercorrection/overcorrection, vision loss, clouding of the cornea, glares, halos, double vision, cataract, and retinal detachment. This procedure may need to be repeated to reposition, replace, or remove the implanted lens, if it moves, is not the right size, or causes severe complications. That being said, the procedure is both repeatable and reversible. Vision stabilizes up to 4 weeks after the procedure.
